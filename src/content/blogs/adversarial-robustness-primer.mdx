## What Are Adversarial Examples?

Deep neural networks are remarkably accurate — until they are not. An **adversarial example** is an input that has been deliberately perturbed by a tiny, often imperceptible amount to cause a model to make the wrong prediction with high confidence.

The classic demonstration: take an image of a panda, add a carefully crafted noise pattern, and a state-of-the-art classifier suddenly outputs "gibbon" — with 99% confidence — even though a human cannot tell the difference.

## Why Does This Happen?

The short answer is that neural networks learn to rely on high-frequency features that are invisible to humans but highly predictive in a statistical sense. Because training optimizes for average-case accuracy, the model's decision boundary can be very close to the data manifold in directions humans do not perceive.

More formally, given a model $f$ and an input $x$ with true label $y$, an adversary finds a perturbation $\delta$ such that:

- $\|\delta\|_p \leq \epsilon$ (the perturbation is small under some norm)
- $f(x + \delta) \neq y$ (the model is fooled)

Common choices are the $L_\infty$ norm (every pixel changes by at most $\epsilon$) and the $L_2$ norm (total Euclidean distance is bounded).

## Common Attack Methods

### FGSM — Fast Gradient Sign Method

The simplest attack, introduced by Goodfellow et al. (2014):

```python
delta = epsilon * torch.sign(grad_x)
x_adv = x + delta
```

One step in the direction that increases the loss the most. Fast, but weak.

### PGD — Projected Gradient Descent

Madry et al. (2018) showed that iterating FGSM with a projection step produces much stronger attacks:

```python
x_adv = x.clone()
for _ in range(steps):
    loss = criterion(model(x_adv), y)
    loss.backward()
    x_adv = x_adv + alpha * x_adv.grad.sign()
    x_adv = project(x_adv, x, epsilon)   # clip back into ball
    x_adv = x_adv.detach().requires_grad_(True)
```

PGD is the de-facto standard for evaluating robustness.

## Defense Strategies

### Adversarial Training

The most reliable defense so far: include adversarial examples in the training set.

```
min_θ  𝔼[(x,y)~D]  max_{‖δ‖≤ε}  L(f_θ(x+δ), y)
```

The inner maximization generates hard examples; the outer minimization learns to classify them correctly. The trade-off is a drop in clean accuracy and higher training cost.

### Certified Defenses

Rather than hoping a defense is robust, **certified** methods provide a mathematical guarantee: for every input within a ball of radius $\epsilon$, the prediction is provably constant. Randomized smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019) is the leading scalable approach.

### Input Preprocessing

Techniques like feature squeezing, JPEG compression, and input denoising can reduce the effectiveness of attacks — but are generally broken by adaptive adversaries who know the preprocessing step.

## Open Problems

- **Robustness vs. accuracy trade-off** — certified robust models are still significantly less accurate on clean data.
- **Semantic attacks** — perturbations that are invisible under $L_p$ norms but clearly visible to humans (e.g., rotation, color shift) remain understudied.
- **Real-world attacks** — most results are in the white-box, digital setting. Physical-world robustness under distribution shift is an open challenge.
